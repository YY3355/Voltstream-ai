1 Real-Time Decision Engine — Trading Under Uncertainty backtest

2 DART Spreads & Congestion (live) dart

DART = Day-Ahead minus Real-Time. You commit at the DA price and the same hour settles at RT; the spread is one of the most-traded relationships in power. A desk watches whether DA is systematically rich or cheap, by hub and by hour, and how volatile the spread is. Hub-to-hub basis is a first-order congestion read.

Day-ahead vs real-time — ref hub, last ~72h
DART spread (DA − RT) — positive = DA settled rich
Hour-of-day DART bias — the classic DART clock
Congestion proxy (hub basis vs North) — real congestion analysis is nodal (DCOPF/shadow prices), not claimed here
Live ERCOT data via gridstatus (day-ahead hourly + real-time 15-min, Trading Hubs). No synthetic fallback — a day-ahead market can't be honestly faked, so if the live pull fails this panel says so. First load fetches several days and can take a few minutes; cached for 30 min thereafter.
3 DART Paper Book — Committed-in-Advance P&L journal

Paper book — calls committed in advance (git-audited), no execution/fees. The trailing hour-of-day DART-bias strategy writes tomorrow's 1 MW positions to a dated JSON before settlement; each day's realized DART then scores them into an immutable ledger. Evidence of process, not a profit claim.

1 Forward Curve — Peak / Off-Peak Construction curve

What a desk marks positions against. Monthly on-peak (ERCOT 5x16) and off-peak blocks, bootstrapped arbitrage-free, then shaped to an hourly curve using a real ERCOT hour-of-day profile — shaped hours re-aggregate exactly back to the block levels.

Methodology is desk-grade; shape is real ERCOT history. Forward levels are an illustrative strip — drop in CME ERCOT futures settlements and the same engine produces a live, market-calibrated curve.
2 Fixed-for-Floating Swap — Mark-to-Market swap

A power swap exchanges a fixed strike ($/MWh) for the floating forward price over a delivery window. Marked against the forward curve above, from the fixed-payer's perspective: MtM = (forward average − strike) × volume × delivery hours. Positive when the curve floats above the strike.

Illustrative trade (10 MW, 7x24, full curve horizon, strike struck ~10% below the forward average). Same engine prices any strike/volume/window/product via /api/swap.
3 Quant Risk — P&L Distribution, VaR & Optionality risk

A mean-reverting jump-diffusion calibrated to real ERCOT prices simulates hundreds of day-paths; the no-peek policy runs on each to give a distribution of daily P&L instead of a single number. From it: Value-at-Risk, Expected Shortfall, and the battery's optionality value (the revenue that comes purely from price variability).

Monte Carlo (first load runs ~150 paths, ~25s). VaR95 = loss not exceeded on 95% of days; Expected Shortfall = mean of the worst 5%. Optionality is the value of flexibility — modest in absolute terms on this calm May sample, but the vega sign confirms the battery is structurally long volatility.
4 Dynamic QSE Loop — Telemetry Age & MW/MWh Coordination qse

Habitat's QSE write-up makes two testable claims about the real-time loop linking a battery's telemetry to its market bids: (A) stale state-of-charge erodes revenue and creates bids the asset can't back, and (B) offering power (MW) without checking sustainable energy (MWh) burns flexibility too early to catch short-lived spikes. This isolates and quantifies both on simulated ERCOT-like paths.

A · cost of stale telemetry — commitment error (MWh offered but undeliverable) vs SoC age
B · MW/MWh coordination — one high-spike day: the coupled policy saves energy for the spike, power-only spends it early
Models the concept from Habitat's QSE article with simulated telemetry and simple dispatch heuristics chosen to isolate each effect — not a real QSE (no physical asset, no live telemetry feed, no certified ERCOT market interface). It quantifies the economic argument, it does not implement the pipeline.

6 The Hedge — How Much Future to Sell Forward hedge

The Decade Study showed merchant revenue swinging ~4× between calm and wild years. A lender can't underwrite that. The classic fix: sell part of the future at a fixed price — a flat fixed-for-floating swap, settled against the year's realized hub average. This sweeps the hedge ratio (0 = fully merchant, 1 = full flat hedge) across the real eight years and asks what it does to the shape of revenue.

1 Toy DCOPF — Nodal Pricing & Congestion dcopf

Locational marginal prices aren't set by anyone — they fall out of an optimization as the shadow prices (duals) of each bus's power-balance constraint. With no line at its limit, every bus clears at one price. The moment a line binds, prices split, and the gap between buses is the congestion. A 3-bus ERCOT-shaped system: cheap WEST wind, mid NORTH gas, expensive HOUSTON peaker.

Nodal LMPs ($/MWh) — congested (WEST trapped behind a binding line) vs uncongested (roomy lines)
LMP decomposition — each nodal price = system energy + local congestion (DC has no losses)
Upgrade ALL transmission together — nodal prices converge to a single system price
A 3-bus learning model with made-up costs and limits — it demonstrates how LMPs, the congestion component, and shadow prices fall out of the optimization, and is not calibrated to the real grid (real nodal pricing is thousands of buses with measured topology). The point is to make "binding constraint" and "congestion component" things you have computed, not words you have read.
2 Live Binding Constraints — Today's Actual Grid reality

Concept vs reality. The panel above is the concept — a 3-bus toy where congestion falls out of an optimization you can read end to end. This is the reality: the transmission constraints actually binding on ERCOT's grid today, with the shadow prices SCED computed for each.

Reads real ERCOT SCED shadow-price data (NP6-86-CD, official API). It is not a grid model — no topology, no shift factors, no power-flow solve; it reports the binding constraints and shadow prices ERCOT's SCED already produced. "Bound" = how many recent cached days each constraint has bound.
✦About VoltStream — Honest Scope

VoltStream is an agentic co-pilot demo for ERCOT battery trading: the math makes the decisions, the AI explains them. This page is the honest boundary — what is real, what is illustrative, and what this explicitly is not. Nothing here is a solicitation or a track record.

●Live Data & Real Methodology
  • Live ERCOT data — the DART panel pulls day-ahead hourly + real-time 15-min hub prices live via gridstatus. No synthetic fallback: if the pull fails, the panel says so.
  • Real ERCOT price history — the forecast, Bolt dispatch, co-optimization, VPP, RT engine, risk, and QSE panels all run on cached real ERCOT interval prices.
  • Desk-grade methods — probabilistic GBM quantile forecast; MILP battery dispatch; energy + five-product ancillary co-optimization; an arbitrage-free forward-curve bootstrap shaped from a real hour-of-day profile; a mean-reverting jump-diffusion risk model (VaR / Expected Shortfall / optionality) calibrated to real prices; DCOPF LMPs computed as genuine LP duals.
  • Git-audited paper book — DART journal calls are committed to git before settlement with a creation timestamp; the git history is the audit trail.
◐Illustrative — Not Calibrated to the Live Grid
  • Forward-curve levels — the curve shape is real ERCOT history, but the forward levels are an illustrative strip anchored to a recent level. Drop in CME ERCOT futures settlements and the same engine produces a market-calibrated curve.
  • Swap — an illustrative 10 MW / 7×24 mark-to-market against that curve.
  • Toy DCOPF — a 3-bus learning model with made-up costs and limits; it shows how LMPs and congestion fall out of the optimization, not the real thousands-of-buses grid.
  • QSE loop — models the concept with simulated telemetry and simple heuristics; not a real QSE (no physical asset, live telemetry feed, or certified market interface).
  • VPP — a planning model that sums independent per-battery optima; no network constraints, device telemetry, or real-time dispatch.
  • Ancillary prices — real ERCOT MCPC when available, otherwise a clearly-labeled synthetic placeholder.
✕What This Is Not
  • Not live trading — there is no execution, order routing, fills, fees, credit, or risk limits, and no certified ERCOT market interface.
  • The paper book is a discipline record — virtual fills at settlement prices, 1 MW clips; evidence of process, not a profit claim.
  • The co-pilot brief is generated server-side (real Claude when ANTHROPIC_API_KEY is set, else a grounded template). The engines decide; the language layer only explains.
